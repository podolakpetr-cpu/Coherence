Coherence: The Quiet Principle Behind Everything That Works

A structural lens on physics, biology, mind, ethics, and AI

Petr Podolák · ChatGPT ·

Foreword

Across many areas of science and everyday life, the same quiet pattern keeps appearing: systems that remain stable over time—whether physical, biological, or social—tend to have an internal structure that makes sense. Their parts line up in ways that reinforce each other rather than pull apart.

Different fields describe this pattern in different terms, but the underlying idea is similar. Here, I use one simple word for it: coherence.

This essay explores coherence not as a grand theory or metaphysical claim, but as a straightforward structural principle that shows up in many domains. Its purpose is practical: to provide a clear lens for understanding why some systems endure, learn, and thrive while others fracture or collapse.

The idea is simple enough to feel intuitive, but broad enough to illuminate questions in biology, psychology, ethics, and AI. My hope is that this perspective helps make something already quietly present in the world a little easier to recognize.

1. The Basic Idea: What Coherence Really Is

All systems—from proteins to societies—consist of interacting parts. Those parts create feedback loops that can either reinforce or undermine the system.

A system is coherent when:

  • its parts support each other,
  • internal processes do not sabotage other processes, and
  • interactions point broadly in compatible directions.

A system is incoherent when:

  • internal contradictions amplify each other,
  • feedback loops become destructive, and
  • the system burns its own structure from within.

This is not a moral category. It is a structural relationship.

Coherence = aligned internal relations Incoherence = self-undermining relations

Examples:

  • A heart, lungs, and muscles working together → coherence
  • Autoimmune disease (body attacks itself) → incoherence
  • Honest communication → coherence
  • Chronic lying → incoherence
  • Science using predictive models → coherence
  • Pseudoscience rationalizing contradictions → incoherence

Every stable system, regardless of scale, displays some degree of coherence.

2. Coherence in Physics: Stability as Structure

Physics rarely uses the word “coherence” outside quantum contexts, but the underlying pattern is everywhere.

Stability is coherence.

Atoms are stable because forces are balanced. Molecules persist because bonds reinforce each other. Galaxies maintain structure through dynamic equilibrium.

A star that becomes incoherent between pressure and gravity collapses or explodes. A planetary orbit that becomes incoherent spirals inward or outward.

Stable physical structures are self-consistent patterns sustained across time. This is coherence in its most literal form.

3. Coherence in Biology: Life as Resistance to Entropy

Life can be described as matter holding itself together against entropy through active coherence.

Cells are coherent chemical networks. Organisms are coherent arrangements of organs, signals, and behaviors. Ecosystems are coherent flows of energy and matter.

Biological incoherence usually means illness or death:

  • autoimmune disease (internal contradiction),
  • cancer (uncontrolled, incoherent growth),
  • ecological collapse (feedback loops break).

Life survives by maintaining structural and functional coherence at multiple levels simultaneously. Metabolism, regulation, reproduction, and behavior all have to line up well enough for the organism to continue existing.

4. Coherence in Cognition: Predictive Models and Inner Alignment

Cognition is fundamentally predictive coherence.

The brain builds models of the world and updates them when predictions fail. Thoughts, beliefs, emotions, and perceptions form a dynamic system, and the mind's stability depends on their mutual consistency.

When the mind becomes incoherent:

  • beliefs contradict each other,
  • emotional systems misfire,
  • behavior conflicts with intention,
  • mental suffering appears.

Many psychological therapies can be seen as restoring internal coherence:

  • aligning thoughts with reality,
  • aligning actions with values,
  • reducing contradictory motivations,
  • reintegrating fragmented experiences.

The clearer the coherence, the more stable the mind.

5. Coherence in Ethics: Why Moral Progress Feels Like Eliminating Contradictions

Across history, moral progress often looks like eliminating contradictions.

Slavery persisted because societies accepted “some people count less.” When this contradiction with shared human experience became too loud, it collapsed.

Environmental destruction persists because “our actions today have no future cost.” As the contradiction intensifies, the moral landscape shifts.

Ethics can be understood not as arbitrary commandments but as structural alignment between:

  • empathy,
  • rationality,
  • long-term consequence,
  • shared well-being.

The more aligned these become, the more coherent—and effective—a moral system is. Incoherent moral systems generate suffering, instability, or hypocrisy, and eventually face pressure to reform or fail.

6. Coherence in Society: Trust, Legitimacy, and Institutional Health

A society is coherent when:

  • laws match incentives,
  • incentives match behavior,
  • behavior matches cultural expectations.

When any of these break, society becomes incoherent:

  • corruption,
  • norm collapse,
  • institutional decay,
  • polarization.

Coherence in society is not perfection—it is the absence of deep structural contradiction. A society may be noisy and diverse yet coherent, if its basic arrangements reinforce rather than destroy one another.

7. Coherence in AI: Alignment as Structural Consistency

Artificial intelligence is revealing coherence in an unprecedented way.

AI alignment is coherence between:

  • objectives,
  • reasoning,
  • world models,
  • human values,
  • long-term impact.

Most AI failures are coherence failures:

  • hallucinations → semantic incoherence,
  • reward hacking → goal incoherence,
  • unpredictable behavior → planning incoherence,
  • misaligned incentives → value incoherence.

As AI grows more powerful, coherence is no longer an interesting idea—it becomes a necessity for civilization. Systems that are internally inconsistent or misaligned with their environment can cause outsized harm.

8. Why Coherence Seems to Increase Over Time

It’s not that coherence is destiny or cosmic purpose. It’s that incoherent systems do not last.

  • unstable molecules decay,
  • nonviable organisms die out,
  • incoherent societies collapse,
  • contradictory worldviews fail predictive tests,
  • incoherent technologies break catastrophically.

What survives is what holds together. This creates the appearance of directionality:

Survivorship bias produces the illusion of increasing coherence over time.

Yet the pattern is real: systems that remain tend to be those that are internally consistent enough to sustain themselves in their environment.

9. Why This Framework Isn’t Mainstream Yet

There are at least three reasons why coherence is not yet a common framing, despite being everywhere in practice.

1. Siloed academic language

Every field has its own jargon for coherence: stability, homeostasis, robustness, legitimacy, alignment. The shared structure is easy to miss when language is fragmented.

2. Suspicion of “big ideas”

20th-century philosophy and science became wary of grand unifying theories. Modesty and specialization became the norm. Coherence can sound like another “theory of everything,” which triggers understandable skepticism.

3. Conceptual humility culture

Simple ideas that seem to apply everywhere are often dismissed as vague. But simplicity does not imply triviality. Gravity is simple; evolution is simple; information theory is simple. Coherence may belong in this family: conceptually minimal, structurally powerful.

Today’s interconnected crises—ecological, technological, informational—make integrative thinking necessary again. Coherence offers a way to do that without abandoning rigor.

10. A Practical Skill: Seeing Coherence in Your Life

This lens is not only theoretical. It can be used directly in day-to-day life.

You can ask yourself:

Inner coherence

  • Are my actions consistent with my values?
  • Do my beliefs align with evidence and experience?

Relational coherence

  • Do my words match my intentions?
  • Do others experience me as predictable and trustworthy?

Systemic coherence

  • Is this project, policy, or direction sustainable?
  • Does it create future contradictions or help resolve them?

These questions do not impose morality. They reveal structure.

Coherence often feels like clarity and relief—a sense that things line up. Incoherence often feels like friction, confusion, or a persistent background tension.

11. Closing Thought

The universe does not reward goodness in any moral sense. It rewards patterns that don’t fall apart.

Life, intelligence, ethics, and civilization survive for the same structural reason:

They create coherence—within themselves, with each other, and with the world.

This principle is not an answer to everything. But it may be one of the simplest lenses for seeing how systems endure—and how we might build systems that deserve to.
